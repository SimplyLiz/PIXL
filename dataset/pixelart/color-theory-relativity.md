# Color Theory for Pixel Artists: It's All Relative

From Pixel Parmesan

## Foundational Concepts

### Color Relativity

Colors don't exist in isolation. The eye uses "chromatic adaptation" to adjust for surrounding light and colors, creating "color constancy" — the ability to recognize an object's color regardless of lighting conditions. This means a single hue can represent different objects in different contexts.

### Three Color Properties (HSV)

- **Hue**: The color family (red, blue, etc.)
- **Saturation**: Purity of the hue
- **Value**: Amount of black or white in a color
- **Perceived Value (Luminance)**: Overall brightness, dependent on all three properties

### Three Theoretical Color Types

1. **Local color**: What an object fundamentally is
2. **Absolute color**: What's actually there (light + local color)
3. **Relative color**: How it appears to the eye in context

## Color Wheels and Gamut

The traditional red-yellow-blue wheel is flawed. The "yurmby" wheel (yellow, red, magenta, blue, cyan, green) better represents the full color spectrum and places RGB primaries evenly.

### Gamut Masking

Select relative primary colors and connect them to create a shape; colors within that shape will appear cohesive because they contain elements of your chosen primaries. This is a powerful technique for creating harmonious limited palettes.

### Subjective Neutral

The geometric center of your color gamut will appear most grey, even if it contains hue. This defines the piece's overall color cast.

## Key Insights

### Hue-Shifting is Natural Light Behavior

"Hue-shifting" isn't a separate technique — it's simply how color naturally behaves under light. All color properties shift simultaneously based on light sources and object properties. The terms "warm" and "cool" lack scientific definition; "relatively warm/cool" is more precise.

### Saturation Follows a Curve

Shadows aren't automatically less saturated than highlights. Saturation follows a curve, peaking in midtones. Different hues reach peak saturation at different values (yellow peaks high, blue peaks low).

## Practical Rendering Techniques

### Color Distribution

Typically use: one dominant color (majority), one accent color (10–20%), secondary colors (remainder). High-contrast accent colors need balancing.

### Grey as Neutralizer

"Greys tend to appear as the relative complement of surrounding colors." A grey near orange appears blue; near blue appears orange. This principle helps create believable color relationships without pushing both colors to extremes.

### Specular Highlights

The color of highlights affects overall tone. Yellow works well due to high luminosity and saturation flexibility, imparting warmth. Other hues require reduced saturation at high values.

### Modeling with Limited Colors

Since pixel art struggles with smooth gradients, compress multiple lighting factors into single shades. Choose tones thoughtfully when combining core shadow with the terminator or center light with halftone.

## Workflow Advice

Rather than predefined palettes, pull colors as you work — from previous pieces, references, or inspirational sources. Adjust colors based on value relationships and context, not symbolic thinking ("the sky isn't always blue").

"Understanding how color behaves in real life, and how it can be used creatively, will help you make informed, intentional decisions."

No single mathematical approach works universally. Understanding foundational rules allows intentional experimentation. Limited palettes encourage discovering unique color combinations through necessity.
